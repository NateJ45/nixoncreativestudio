## The brief

West Chester Preschool has been running since 1969, which makes it older than most of the parents touring it. It is a play-based cooperative preschool housed inside Crestview Presbyterian Church in West Chester, Ohio, serving kids from age two through pre-K. "Cooperative" is the important word: the families who enroll are also the ones who help run the place, rotating through classroom helper shifts and serving on the board.

The website had to do two jobs that pull in different directions. It needed to win new families, parents researching their child's first preschool, often on a phone, comparing a handful of options. And it needed to serve the families already enrolled, who run the school's day-to-day operations and need a reliable place for schedules, the directory, and class photos. One site, two audiences, both of them busy.

## The approach

For prospective families, the first hurdle is the word "co-op" itself. A first-time preschool parent does not automatically know that a cooperative preschool means more involvement, more community, and usually lower tuition than a standard daycare. So the public site leads by explaining the model plainly, then makes the path to a tour short. A parent should be able to understand what makes WCP different, see the real cost, and book a visit without making a phone call.

Tuition is published right on the site, starting at $70 a month for the Twos class. That was a deliberate call. Cost is the first question every preschool parent has, and hiding it behind a "contact us" form reads as evasive to someone comparing four schools in one evening. Showing the numbers up front builds trust and filters for families the co-op model actually fits.

For the platform, Squarespace was the right choice for an organization run entirely by volunteers. Leadership turns over as kids age out, so the site has to be maintainable by whoever inherits it, not by a developer on retainer. Squarespace handles the marketing pages and a small store for fees and supplies, and its built-in editor means the next board can keep things current.

For current families, we built the portal section. The school already kept its operational data in tools it used every day: Google Calendar for events, Google Sheets for helper rotations, Google Photos for class albums. Rather than rebuild all of that inside the website, we connected to it with custom code blocks. When a board member updates the rotation spreadsheet or adds a photo album, the portal reflects it on the next visit. Nobody has to log into the website to keep it accurate.

The full build, including the pieces above:

- A public marketing and enrollment site: who we are, why WCP, co-op life, an honest FAQ, and individual pages for the Twos, Threes, and Pre-K classes
- An "A Day at WCP" page that walks a prospective parent through the actual daily schedule, the question behind the question for anyone leaving their two-year-old somewhere new
- A short tour-to-enroll path: schedule a visit, enroll, and a tuition page that publishes real monthly pricing instead of hiding it
- A family portal connected live to the school's existing Google Workspace: event calendar, helper rotation schedules, the directory for all enrolled families, and class photo albums
- A Squarespace store for tuition payments, fees, and supplies
- Social feeds surfacing summer playdates so the community stays connected through the off-season
